# Beta dispersion lacks interpretability for differential stochastic dispersion analyses


## Abstract


## Main

In ecology and microbiology, a fundamental goal is to quantify how communities differ in composition and heterogeneity. Beta-diversity, the degree of compositional differentiation between communities, has become a cornerstone metric for understanding how species assemblages vary across space, time, environmental gradients, or host states. Distance-based permutational tests (e.g., PERMANOVA) have become standard tools for testing hypotheses about compositional shifts between groups. Beta-diversity is typically quantified through distance metrics applied to community abundances. Some of the most common distance metrics are Bray–Curtis, Jaccard, Aitchison, and Hellinger. Each metric underlies specific assumptions about data properties and which aspect of the data best reflects the biological question at hand [REF]. The challenge in choosing a distance metric stems from the high-dimensional, sparse, and compositional nature of ecology and microbiome data [10]. These metrics depend on a chain of analytical choices, normalisation, transformation, and ordination geometry that determine which biological mechanisms (changes in abundant taxa, changes in rare taxa, phylogenetic turnover, presence/absence shifts, compositional constraints) will manifest as diversity or dispersion differences. Distances differ in what they emphasise (abundance versus presence/absence, rare versus common taxa), what preprocessing they require (normalisation, transformation), and how they behave under compositional constraints and zeros. 

Bray–Curtis is an abundance-based dissimilarity computed from sample-wise abundances (commonly counts after normalisation or relative abundances), and it does not incorporate shared absences into the dissimilarity[3]. Jaccard and related Sørensen family metrics quantify overlap in community membership and are often applied to binary presence/absence data, but abundance-weighted or quantitative variants are also used with non-binary abundance or relative-abundance profiles [4]. Euclidean distance on transformed data is also widely used, where the transformation is chosen so that Euclidean geometry is meaningful for community data. A common example is the Hellinger transform, which applies a square-root transform to relative abundances, after which Euclidean distance is used for ordination and related analyses[5]. The Aitchison distance is defined for compositions in the simplex and is commonly computed as the Euclidean distance in centred log-ratio (CLR) space. Because log-ratio transforms are undefined at zero, practical implementations require a zero-handling strategy, such as pseudocount addition or zero replacement [6]. Robust Aitchison approaches have been proposed that modify the log-ratio representation or employ robust, sparse factorisation to improve stability under sparsity and outliers in microbiome tables [7]. Phylogenetic distances, notably unweighted and weighted UniFrac, incorporate evolutionary relationships among taxa via a reference phylogeny; unweighted UniFrac is defined on presence/absence, whereas weighted UniFrac additionally incorporates relative abundance[8].

While beta-diversity addresses whether groups differ in their mean composition, an equally important question concerns the variability of communities within a condition, habitat, host state, or treatment. Beta dispersion, the multivariate analogue of variance in community composition, summarises within-group heterogeneity as distances of samples to their group centroid in the space induced by a chosen dissimilarity metric. Dispersion analyses probe community variability, stability, and heterogeneity, and have become central to understanding resilience under disturbance, the emergence of alternative community states, and the mechanisms of microbial community assembly. In practice, beta dispersion is used to test whether a perturbation increases variability in community composition, whether disease is associated with increased inter-individual heterogeneity, or whether interventions stabilise communities by reducing variability. These questions complement traditional beta-diversity analyses: where PERMANOVA tests whether groups differ in centroid location[1], PERMDISP tests whether groups differ in spread around their centroids[2]. PERMDISP-based beta dispersion is designed as a distance-based, model-agnostic way to test whether groups differ in multivariate dispersion in a chosen dissimilarity space and is commonly implemented via vegan::betadisper with permutation assessment using vegan::permutest[2]. 

In community ecology, dispersion has been interpreted as evidence for environmental filtering (reduced dispersion) versus niche diversification or disturbance-driven heterogeneity (increased dispersion). In host-associated microbiomes, increased dispersion has been used to operationalise the “Anna Karenina principle” of microbiomes, in which stressed or diseased hosts exhibit more heterogeneous microbiome states than healthy hosts[9]. In intervention studies, reduced dispersion is sometimes interpreted as community stabilisation, whereas increased dispersion can be interpreted as destabilisation or divergent responses across individuals. These interpretations are appealing because they map a multivariate object (community composition) to a scalar summary that can be compared across groups and plotted alongside other outcomes. 

However, the inferential leap from “distance-to-centroid is larger” to “stochastic dispersion is larger” is not automatic. Beta dispersion is a property of the chosen distance representation and of the ordination geometry used to compute centroids and distances. In microbiome settings, where composition, sparsity, and mean–variance structure are intrinsic, the mapping between beta dispersion and underlying biological variability may be indirect, distance-dependent, and confounded by systematic shifts in mean composition. Because both centroid location and within-group spread are computed from the same distance matrix and their relationship is geometric, the interpretation of both beta-diversity and beta dispersion depends critically on the distance metric used to define between-sample dissimilarity. This relationship also matters because PERMANOVA is sensitive to differences in within-group dispersion[1], so a significant PERMANOVA result may reflect separation of group centroids, differences in dispersion, or a combination of both.

Here, using a generative, parametric compositional Bayes model, we benchmark the sensitivity of betadispersion to pure compositional changes and pure dispersion changes. We, provide quantitative evidence that beta dispersion does not provide a direct, interpretable estimate of stochastic dispersion in the generative sense; that is, variability of counts or proportions around an expected composition under a statistical model. We implicitly highlight the need for probabilistic models of dispersion that can be interpreted directly under testable assumptions about distributions, abundances, and variability. These issues motivate a careful re-examination of beta dispersion as a surrogate for stochastic dispersion, and the development of benchmarks that explicitly separate changes in mean composition from changes in dispersion under realistic microbiome regimes. In this work, we systematically evaluate common distance constructions used as inputs to PERMDISP and quantify when and how they produce false signals of dispersion under pure compositional shifts, and how their sensitivity changes under controlled dispersion perturbations. Our goal is to clarify what beta dispersion measures in practice and to motivate more interpretable approaches for differential stochastic dispersion analysis in high-dimensional compositional data.

To provide a robust evaluation of the ability of beta dispersion to detect differences in abundance versus stochastic dispersion, we simulated compositional data independently controlling for location and stochastic dispersion (Figure 1A). To ensure relevance to the field, we simulated data using a generative model trained on experimental data. First, we used the generative probabilistic Bayesian framework (sccomp [REF]) to model compositional and noise properties of the reference HMP2 gut microbiome cohort [11,12]. Then, based on the learned posterior joint distribution, we simulated diverse scenarios, tuning differential composition and variability, and noise. We used the IBD condition as a unique factor.

### Beta dispersion detects pure stochastic differential overdispersion, but with centroid shift

To establish a positive control, we tested whether PERMDISP can reliably detect genuine differences in stochastic dispersion in the absence of a difference in mean composition. We simulated compositional count tables with identical mean composition across conditions but with increasing differential dispersion (ranging from 0 to 1 log-dispersion units). For each simulated dataset, we computed five commonly used inter-sample distances (Bray–Curtis, Jaccard, Hellinger, Aitchison, and robust Aitchison) and applied PERMDISP to test for differences in beta dispersion. A representative simulation at delta = 0.5 confirmed that both conditions shared the same taxon abundance profiles, with overlapping per-taxon means and confidence intervals (Fig. 1C, left). Alpha diversity analyses show a similar evenness matching the unchanged mean composition; on the contrary, the richness was decreased overall for the IBD condition, due to the increased dispersion (Figure 1D). The distance-to-centroid distributions (Figure 1E) show the ability of all metrics to detect the increased dispersion in the IBD condition, although the magnitude of separation varied by metric. Similarly, PCoA plots (Figure 1F) show an overall higher distance-to-centroid for the IBD condition in the first two dimensions, particularly for the Aitchison and Hellinger methods. We then tested the sensitivity to increasing differential stochastic dispersion. This analysis showed, as expected, a decreased false-negative rate as the true differential pverdispersion increased for all methods, independently on the filtering strategy used. The Aitchison method and its robust implementation showed the highest sensitivity, followed by Hellingen. On the contrary, Bray-Curtis shows the overall highest false-negative rate, followed by Jaccard.

The PCoA also shows that a pure differential dispersion signal leads to a centroid shift, especially for Bray-Curtis and Aitchison methods (Figure 1F). We then tested if the centroid distance across methods would increase at the increase of simulated overdispersion (Figure 1G). Tracing the percentage centroid shift over the average hull radius, we observed that there is a positive association between the centroid distance and the simulated overdispersion (p-value < 0.001). We observed this shift for all tested filtering strategies and particularly for Bray-Curtis and Hellinger methods, which have a near doubling along the simulated differential dispersion spectrum (Figure 1H).

These results confirm that PERMDISP can detect true differential stochastic dispersion, but they reveal that its effective sensitivity is a property of the distance construction, not solely of the underlying biological signal. This distance-dependence means that a centroid shift can occur without real mean compositional changes.

### Beta dispersion is biasedly significant under pure compositional shifts

In order to evaluate the specificity of PERMDISP, that is, its ability to remain negative when dispersion is truly equal between conditions, we tested its behaviour under the complementary scenario: differential abundance without any differential dispersion. We simulated count tables in which a controlled number of taxa (ranging from 0 to 40) were differentially abundant between conditions, with effect sizes sampled from the empirically observed distribution of significant condition-associated composition shifts (Figure 1B). In contrast, we held dispersion parameters identical across conditions. We considered two settings: one in which overdispersion was homogeneous across taxa to prioritise experimental interpretability, and one in which taxa exhibited realistic heterogeneity in overdispersion around the mean–dispersion trend, as estimated from HMP2. 

A representative simulation with 40 differentially abundant taxa confirmed that taxon abundance profiles diverged substantially between conditions (Fig. 2A). Alpha diversity analyses (Figure 2B) showed that the IBD condition shows the highest average evenness, due to the specific differential abundance pattern (e.g. decrease of the most abundant taxa). Average richness was also increased due to the lack of abundance increases among rare taxa. Beta dispersion analysis of the representative simulation (Figure 2C) shows clear differences in distance-to-centroid distributions across all methods, with the Bray-Curtis method less pronounced in this example. The PCoA (Figure 1D) confirms that, while for minimal differential abundance the area inside the convex hull between conditions is similar, and centroids are proximal, both are diverse as we increase the number of differentially abundant taxa. This is particularly evident for the Aitchison methods for the 10 and 40 differentially abundant taxa examples.

We then tested the specificity of PERMDISP by sweeping the number of differentially abundant taxa (ranging from 0 to 40) and the distance construction used (Figure 2E). In the no-differential-abundance (negative-control) regimen, we observed the expected 5% false-positive rate across all methods. However, when the number of differentially abundant taxa increases, the false positive rate increases for all methods and in all filtering strategies, particularly for the Aitchison and Jaccard methods. The method that is moreinsesnitive to compositional changes is Bray-Curtis, whose false-positive rate remains below 15%. We then tested this trend in case when not all taxa have the same baseline dispersion, with such heterogeneity estimated from HMP2. While the rank of the less specific method stays consistent, all methods perform worse for all three filtering strategies, with a false-positive rate ranging from XXX% to XX% across methods for the negative control at no differential abundance (Figure 2G). 

Although Bray-Curtis appear to be robust to compositional shifts, this goes at the price of being the worst performing in our sensitivity analyses (pure differential overdispersion; Figure 2G). Overall, we observed a clear trade-off between sensitivity and specificity across distance constructions, with no single method achieving simultaneously low false-positive and false-negative rates (Figure 3G).

These results suggest that beta dispersion is generally not specific to stochastic dispersion changes, exhibiting a high false-positive rate under pure compositional changes. Additionally, they show that a compromise exists across methods in terms of sensitivity and specificity, with no single method occupying the ideal corner of low false-positive and false-negative rates simultaneously.

Beta dispersion has become a cornerstone metric in ecology and microbiome research, widely used to quantify community heterogeneity, test hypotheses about variability differences between groups, and operationalise concepts such as community stability and the "Anna Karenina principle" in host-associated microbiomes. However, our systematic evaluation reveals that beta dispersion, as implemented through PERMDISP, conflates stochastic dispersion with compositional shifts in ways that depend on the chosen distance metric. We demonstrate three key findings. First, changes in mean community composition produce spurious dispersion signals across all distance constructions tested, with false-positive rates reaching 15–40% under realistic differential-abundance scenarios. Second, pure dispersion changes induce centroid shifts that can be misinterpreted as compositional differences, particularly for Bray–Curtis and Aitchison distances. Third, the sensitivity–specificity trade-off is inherent to distance-based dispersion summaries: metrics that are sensitive to true dispersion differences (Aitchison, robust Aitchison) also exhibit elevated false-positive rates under compositional shifts, whereas metrics that are robust to compositional artefacts (Bray–Curtis) exhibit reduced sensitivity to genuine dispersion differences. These findings have implications for the interpretation of existing beta dispersion studies. When dispersion differences are reported alongside compositional shifts, it becomes difficult to determine whether observed heterogeneity reflects genuine stochastic variability differences or systematic shifts in mean composition. The distance-dependent nature of these artefacts means that the same underlying biological signal can produce quantitatively different conclusions depending on the chosen metric, complicating cross-study comparisons and meta-analyses. Moving forward, the field would benefit from model-based approaches that explicitly parameterise composition and variability as distinct, testable quantities. Probabilistic models of compositional count data would provide interpretable parameters for mean composition and stochastic dispersion, enable direct hypothesis tests on dispersion parameters, and allow validation of modelling assumptions through diagnostics and posterior predictive checks. These methods separate composition from variability by construction, provide interpretable effect sizes with uncertainty quantification, and make testable assumptions about data-generating processes that can be validated or rejected. Such approaches offer a path toward more interpretable and specific inference about differential stochastic dispersion in compositional data.

## Methods

### Fitting data

To ground simulations in empirically realistic microbiome regimes, we parameterised our simulator using a Bayesian generative fit from sccomp to the HMP2 gut microbiome cohort (Healthy vs IBD) [11]. The sccomp fit was generated from genus-level count tables built from HMP2 taxonomic profiles (taxonomic_profiles.biom) and sample metadata (hmp2_metadata.csv) (see lejing_data/hmp2.Rmd): taxa were aggregated to genus, samples with zero total counts were removed, and genera with all-zero counts were removed. Disease status was encoded as a two-level factor (group in {Healthy, IBD}, with Healthy as the reference level).

We fitted sccomp (R 4.5.0; sccomp 2.1.25) with composition and variability models both specified as ~ 1 + group using HMC/NUTS (sccomp_estimate(..., formula_composition = ~ 1 + group, formula_variability = ~ 1 + group, sample = "sample", cell_group = "cell_group", abundance = "count", inference_method = "hmc", bimodal_mean_variability_association = TRUE, pass_fit = TRUE)). The stored posterior fit is a cmdstanr::CmdStanMCMC object (cmdstanr 0.9.0; Stan 2.36.0) run with 1 chain, 300 warmup iterations and 666 sampling iterations (seed 96414; adapt_delta = 0.8, max_treedepth = 10, metric = "diag_e", threads_per_chain = 3).

From the fitted HMP2 model we obtained the empirical sample and taxon counts (N = 178, M = 384) and used posterior summaries of taxon-level composition effects to parameterise simulations. Specifically, baseline abundances were drawn from the distribution of per-taxon intercept effects (c_effect for parameter == "(Intercept)"), and differential-abundance effect sizes were drawn from the distribution of per-taxon group-associated composition effects (c_effect for parameter == "groupIBD"). To represent empirically supported non-null shifts, differential-abundance effect sizes were sampled from the subset of group-associated effects whose 95% credible intervals excluded 0 (c_lower > 0 or c_upper < 0).

We also used the fitted mean–dispersion relationship from sccomp, which is parameterised in terms of precision via prec_coeff. Because precision is approximately inversely proportional to overdispersion, we mapped the fitted mean–precision relationship onto the mean–overdispersion parameterisation used by our simulator using the approximation log(sigma) ≈ -log(precision). Concretely, letting prec_coeff[1] and prec_coeff[2] denote the intercept and slope of the mean–precision relationship on the log scale, we set the simulator’s mean–overdispersion slope to k_real = E[prec_coeff[2]] and the baseline overdispersion intercept to c_real = -E[prec_coeff[1]], and we used prec_sd as the taxon-to-taxon dispersion heterogeneity term when simulating heterogeneous-overdispersion regimes.

### Simulation

We generated synthetic count tables with a compositional beta–binomial simulation framework that produces overdispersed counts under a compositional constraint. Each dataset comprised two groups with equal numbers of samples, a fixed number of taxa (capped at 200 for computational tractability), and library sizes drawn from a distribution matched to the empirical dataset. Baseline composition parameters were sampled from the empirical intercept distribution and centered to satisfy the compositional constraint.

We simulated two regimes to disentangle sensitivity to true dispersion differences from susceptibility to compositional differences. In the differential abundance (DA) regime, we introduced composition shifts in a controlled number of taxa while holding dispersion equal between groups. We swept the number of DA taxa from 0 to 40 and repeated simulations across independent random seeds. We considered two DA settings: one with no additional taxon-to-taxon heterogeneity in overdispersion and one including taxon-level heterogeneity around the mean–dispersion trend, parameterized by the dispersion variability inferred from sccomp. For each simulated dataset we optionally applied abundance filtering, removing taxa whose mean relative abundance across samples was below 0.1% and repeated the analysis with 0.5% for comparative purpose, before computing distances.

In the differential overdispersion (DO) regime, we simulated no differential abundance (all composition slopes set to zero) while introducing between-group differences in dispersion by offsetting the dispersion intercept between groups on a log scale. We evaluated a grid of dispersion offsets (including a null offset of 0) and repeated simulations across independent random seeds. Unless stated otherwise, we did not include additional taxon-to-taxon random heterogeneity in overdispersion in this regime, in order to isolate the effect of a controlled between-group dispersion shift. Abundance filtering at a 0.1% mean relative abundance threshold was applied prior to distance computation.

### Analysis

For each simulated count table we constructed inter-sample dissimilarities using five distance constructions commonly used as inputs to PERMDISP: Bray–Curtis, Jaccard (computed after total-sum normalization), Hellinger–Euclidean (Hellinger transform followed by Euclidean distance), Aitchison (CLR-based), and robust Aitchison. For Aitchison-based distances, zeros were handled by adding a small pseudocount prior to log-ratio transformation. These distance matrices were then analysed using PERMDISP as implemented in vegan::betadisper.

PERMDISP embeds samples in principal coordinate space derived from the distance matrix and quantifies dispersion as the distance from each sample to its group centroid. We tested for between-group differences in dispersion using permutation of group labels via vegan::permutest applied to the fitted betadisper object. For figure-level analyses we used an adaptive permutation strategy, increasing permutations in batches until the Monte Carlo standard error of the p-value estimate dropped below a prespecified target (or until a maximum number of permutations was reached), and we recorded the achieved permutation count and Monte Carlo standard error. For large parameter sweeps we used a fixed number of permutations per replicate to enable dense grids while keeping inference consistent across methods and conditions. All tests used a nominal two-sided significance level of $\alpha = 0.05$.

To summarize performance across simulation replicates, we computed the false positive rate (FPR) in the DA regime as the fraction of replicates with p < 0.05 when the true dispersion difference was zero, and the false negative rate (FNR) in the DO regime as the fraction of replicates with p > 0.05 when the true dispersion difference was non-zero. As an interpretable effect-size proxy, we also summarized the between-group difference in mean distance-to-centroid returned by PERMDISP.

Importantly, abundance filtering changes the dimensionality of the data: in some simulated datasets, a stringent mean-abundance threshold can remove most taxa and leave only a very small number of features. This matters because some distance constructions are not defined (or become uninformative) in extremely low dimensions. For example, log-ratio based distances such as CLR/Aitchison require at least two taxa to form log-ratios, and when only one or two taxa remain the resulting distance matrix can be degenerate, making the principal coordinate analysis step underlying PERMDISP ill-posed or trivial. To ensure that all distances and the subsequent PERMDISP geometry were well-defined and comparable across methods, we restricted distance-based analyses to datasets with at least three taxa remaining after filtering.

### Visualisation

To illustrate how distance constructions affect PERMDISP geometry, we visualized representative simulations using principal coordinate (PCoA) scatter plots of sample scores with group centroids overlaid, together with boxplots of sample-to-centroid distances by group. To contextualize compositional structure and filtering effects, we additionally visualized taxon abundance profiles using per-taxon mean relative abundances with 95% intervals and plotted alpha-diversity summaries for representative datasets. Alpha diversity was computed in R from the simulated count tables as follows: richness was defined as the number of taxa with non-zero counts per sample (observed richness), Shannon diversity was computed from within-sample relative abundances p as H = -sum(p log p) over taxa with p > 0, and evenness was reported as Pielou’s evenness J = H / log(richness). Richness versus evenness was visualised using ggplot2, with 95% confidence ellipses computed using ggplot2::stat_ellipse (normal-theory ellipse at level 0.95). Summary performance curves were plotted as FPR across the DA sweep and as FNR across the DO dispersion-offset sweep, with consistent method-specific color mapping across figures.
